Rules every person must follow
These rules apply to every site, every day, for every person on Chislett Farms property.
  • Sign in and sign out every day. Your supervisor must know you are on site.
  • Wear the correct Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) for your task at all times.
  • Follow all instructions from your supervisor. If you are unsure, stop and ask. You will not get in trouble for asking.
  • Keep your work area clean and tidy. Pick up rubbish and store equipment safely.
  • Drive or operate vehicles at 10 km/h maximum speed on all farm sites.
  • Do not use your mobile phone while operating machinery or driving on site.
  • Only operate equipment or machinery that you have been trained and authorised to use.
  • No alcohol, illegal drugs, or weapons are permitted on any Chislett Farms site at any time.
  • Do not enter restricted or locked areas without permission from your supervisor.
  • NO GLASS OR CONTAMINANTS of any kind are permitted in the Packing Shed, Nursery, or on any farm site. This includes glass bottles, broken containers, or foreign objects that could contaminate produce. This is a strict food safety rule.
Breaking these rules may result in disciplinary action, removal from site, or where required by law, reporting to authorities.

Working in the heat
Farm work in hot weather can be dangerous. Heat illness can happen quickly, especially if you are new to the job or working on a very hot day.
  • Drink water regularly. At least one cup every 20 minutes when it is hot.
  • Wear a hat, long sleeves, and sunscreen when working outdoors.
  • Take shade breaks when your supervisor tells you to, or when you feel unwell.
  • Tell your supervisor if you or a workmate feel dizzy, sick, confused, or stop sweating.
  • Do not work through heat illness symptoms. They can become life-threatening very quickly.
  • Check on new workers and PALM scheme workers during their first week. They may not be used to the heat yet.
Signs of heat illness: headache, dizziness, weakness, heavy sweating, nausea, or confusion. If someone collapses or stops making sense, call 000 immediately.
Supervisors: On days over 35 degrees Celsius, additional rest breaks and cool water must be provided. This is a legal requirement.
Scene 6 of 12
Working safely with chemicals
Chislett Farms uses chemicals for crop protection and growing. These must be handled safely to protect you, your workmates, and our produce.
  • Only handle chemicals if you have been trained and authorised to do so.
  • Always wear the PPE listed on the chemical label: gloves, mask, and eye protection.
  • Read the label and Safety Data Sheet (SDS) before using any chemical. Ask your supervisor if you cannot read it.
  • Never mix chemicals unless specifically instructed by your supervisor.
  • Store chemicals in their correct, labelled containers in the locked chemical storage area only.
  • Never eat, drink, or smoke near chemicals or chemical storage areas.
  • Do not enter a recently sprayed area until the re-entry period has passed. Your supervisor will tell you when it is safe.
  • If you spill a chemical or feel unwell after contact, tell your supervisor immediately and wash the affected area with water.
Chemical exposure can cause serious health problems. If you have any reaction to skin, eyes, or breathing, tell your supervisor right away and seek first aid.

Scene 8 of 12
Lifting and carrying safely
Many farm injuries happen from lifting incorrectly. Always use safe lifting techniques to protect your back, shoulders, and knees.
  • 1Plan before you lift. Is the load too heavy? Do you need help or a trolley?
  • 2Stand close to the load. Keep your feet shoulder-width apart.
  • 3Bend your knees, not your back. Keep your back straight.
  • 4Hold the load close to your body. Grip it firmly.
  • 5Lift slowly using your legs. Do not jerk or twist.
  • 6If you need to turn, move your feet. Do not twist your back.
  • 7Set the load down carefully, bending your knees again.
If a load is too heavy for one person, ask for help or use a trolley, pallet jack, or forklift. No load is worth injuring yourself over.
Report any pain or strain immediately, even if it seems minor. Injuries caught early heal faster and prevent serious long-term damage.

What to do in an emergency
An emergency can happen at any time. Knowing what to do before it happens could save a life, including yours.
EMERGENCY NUMBER
000
Fire, Ambulance, Police
  • 1Stay calm. Stop work immediately.
  • 2Move yourself and others away from danger, only if it is safe to do so.
  • 3Call 000. Tell them your location, what happened, and how many people are injured.
  • 4Call or notify your supervisor straight away.
  • 5Do not move an injured person unless they are in immediate further danger.
  • 6Send someone to the site gate to direct emergency services in.
  • 7Do not leave the scene unless told to by emergency services or your supervisor.
Know the location of: first aid kit, fire extinguisher, emergency assembly point, and nearest exit. Ask your supervisor to show you on Day 1.
Scene 10 of 12
See it. Report it. Fix it.
All injuries, near-misses, and hazards must be reported, no matter how small. Early reporting prevents serious accidents.
  • Injury: Tell your supervisor immediately. Get first aid. Complete an incident report on the same day.
  • Near-miss: Something almost went wrong but did not. Report it before the end of your shift.
  • Hazard: Something that could cause harm. Report to your supervisor as soon as you see it.
  • Property damage: Any damage to equipment, vehicles, buildings, or produce must be reported immediately.
  • Illness: If you feel unwell at work, tell your supervisor. Do not come to work if you are sick with a fever, vomiting, or diarrhea.
You will NEVER get in trouble for reporting an incident, hazard, or near-miss. Reporting is part of your job and helps keep everyone safe. You are protected by law when you report safety issues.
Confidential matters, safety concerns, or complaints can be raised directly with the HR and Quality Assurance Manager: Gretchen at gretchen@chislettfarms.com.au
